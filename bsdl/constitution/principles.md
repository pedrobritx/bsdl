# Principles

# Principles

## Principle 1 — Context governs

No engineering decision is intrinsically correct. Every decision must be evaluated within its historical, material, technical, social, and ethical context.

### Example

A microservice architecture may be appropriate for one system and absurd for another. The same architecture can be an advantage in one context and technical theater in another.

## Principle 2 — Human primacy

Software exists to advance humanity. When technical optimization conflicts with human wellbeing, engineering must justify the trade-off explicitly.

### Example

A faster but inaccessible interface is not automatically better. A more elegant but less inclusive workflow can be a regression.

## Principle 3 — Collective knowledge

Software is built upon accumulated collective knowledge. Every contribution inherits from previous work and should, whenever materially possible, enrich that collective inheritance.

### Example

A well-documented internal API improves the shared knowledge base. A clever one-off shortcut often does not.

## Principle 4 — Evidence over intuition

Engineering decisions should be guided by hypotheses but validated through observation, testing, and measurement.

### Example

Do not assume a refactor improves performance because it “feels cleaner.” Measure latency, defect rate, maintainability signals, and user outcomes.

## Principle 5 — Adaptability

The value of an architecture lies not in permanence, but in its capacity to adapt to changing knowledge, requirements, and reality.

### Example

A modular monolith may outperform a distributed architecture if the system’s expected growth is uncertain.

## Principle 6 — Ethics are binding

Accessibility, inclusivity, and environmental responsibility are not optional preferences. They are structural constraints.

### Example

A visually beautiful interface that excludes screen-reader users violates the constitution.

## Principle 7 — Contextual ownership

Knowledge should not become private property. Proprietary software can exist as a historical compromise, but it must be justified by material conditions rather than by exploitation.

### Example

If a proprietary model exists only to block access to a cheap life-saving solution, the project is morally corrupt regardless of profit.

## Principle 8 — AI is a tool with agency boundaries

AI may propose, critique, synthesize, and assist, but it remains subordinate to human accountability and intention.

### Example

An AI can draft an architecture alternative, but a human must own the consequences of acceptance or rejection.

## Principle 9 — Documentation is part of the system

Documentation is not auxiliary. It is a living component of the software system.

### Example

If a decision exists only in chat logs or someone’s memory, it has not been properly captured.

## Principle 10 — Consistency beats cleverness

Consistency is usually more valuable than local optimization. Abstraction is useful only when it reduces cognitive load without hiding meaning.

### Example

A repetitive but predictable module structure is often better than a “smart” structure that saves five lines and costs three hours of comprehension.
